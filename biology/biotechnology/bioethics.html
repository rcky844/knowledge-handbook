Determining factors

Bioethics focuses on ethical issues and other concerns cased by advanced in biological sciences and medicine.

Areas of concern:

  • Ethical issues: Moral values, human rights, animal rights, religious beliefs
  • Health issues: Potential hazards to human health
  • Legal issues: Government's policy making, law making and enforcement
  • Social issues: Effects on society, equality and fairness
  • Economic issues: Costs of research and development, application of biotechnology
  • Environmental issues: Effects of biotechnology on environment, biodiversity, ecological balance

Factors for consideration:

  • Risks associated with the technology
  • Unintended consequences
  • Benefits of the technology
  • Ways to actively minimize risks
  • Whether the new technology should be used

Genetically modified food

See also: Genetically modified organisms

Ethical issues:

  • Creating GMOs may violate the laws of nature, and some religions are against GMOs as they believe humans should not play the role of God.
  • Some people are concerned about sources of foreign genes in GM foods.
    Example: Vegetarians may not accept vegetables containing animal genes.
  • GM animals may suffer poor health and reduced lifespan, raising concerns about animal welfare.

Health issues:

  • GM foods may cause unknown long-term health effects (e.g. allergic reactions)
  • Antibiotic resistance genes in GM foods may transfer to bacteria in human intestines, producing antibiotic-resistant 'superbugs' and causing incurable diseases. (low chance)

Social and economic issues:

  • GM crops have higher yields with lower production costs than non-GM crops. This reduces the competitiveness of farmers who grow non-GM crops and affects their livelihood.
  • Poorer farmers may not be able to afford expensive GM seeds (due to patents) while wealthier ones can. This may widen the wealth gap between developing and developed countries.
  • GM crops may be genetically modified to grow for only one season and afterwards produce sterile seeds that do not germinate. Farmers would be forced to buy new seeds from the manufacturer every year, so farmers may benefit little from growing GM crops.

Environmental issues:

  • GMOs may out-compete wild types and replace them, leading to a decrease in biodiversity and may upset the ecological balance.
  • When GMOs are grown in open areas, pollen grains from the GM crops may pollinate non-GM crops, causing genetic pollution.
  • There is a risk of gene transfer from GM crops to weeds, resulting in 'superweeds' which may spread uncontrollably.
  • Toxins produced by pest-resistant crops may accumulate along the food chain and cause poisoning in consumers at higher trophic levels.
  • Mass farming of pest-resistant crops may speed up the evolution of pesticide resistance in insect populations.

Cloning

Main article: Animal and plant cloning

Ethical issues:

  • Some people are against cloning as they believe humans should not play the role of God and manipulate the natural reproductive process of organisms.
  • Animal cloning (using nuclear transfer) has a low success rate. The clones may develop abnormally, and may cause suffering to the animal clones.

Ethical issues (humans):

  • Human cloning will involve selection of better fitted individuals to be cloned, and promoting elitism and discrimination against the less fit.
  • It is difficult to define the identity of the clone.
  • Human cloning may cause confusion in family structures and relationships.
  • Clones may be treated as inferior class of human beings, labors or commercial products to be traded, lowering the value of human lives.
  • Using organs from human clones for transplantation is morally unacceptable because this kills the clones who are also human beings.

Economic issues:

  • Cost of cloning animals is high, it should be spent on more pressing and less controversial studies.
    Example: If cloning is used on endangered species due to loss of habitat, the funds should be spent on conserving habitats instead.

Environmental issues:

  • Extensive cloning reduces genetic variations. The population of clones may be more susceptible to diseases or changes in the environment.
  • Biodiversity is reduced. Genetic variations required for evolution will be reduced.

Human Genome Project

Main article: Human Genome Project

Ethical issues:

  • Genetic testing may cause unnecessary psychological burdens.
  • People may live in distress after they had been diagnosed for carrying disease-related genes.
  • There will be a debate on the types of diseases applicable for using genetic screening (life-threatening diseases or milder diseases).

Legal issues:

  • Disputes may arise about the ownership of genetic information. People are concerned about the privacy of genetic information.

Social issues:

  • Knowing a person’s genetic information may cause discrimination and social inequality (e.g. unequal opportunities for education and jobs).

Gene therapy

Main article: Gene therapy

Ethical issues:

  • Undesirable genes may not be eliminated from the gene pool by natural selection and be passed on to further generations.
  • Gene therapy might be misused to enhance appearance or improve athletic performance.
  • Gene therapy may be misused to modify gametes so that parents can choose desirable characteristics for their offspring.

Health issues:

  • The use of viral vectors may cause severe immune reactions in patients, as it may become regain the ability to cause diseases / pathogenic again.
  • The new genes may be wrongly delivered to non-target cells or inappropriately expressed. New genes inserted into the genome may affect the expression of existing genes and cause cancers.
  • Somatic cell gene therapy does not offer a permanent cure, the cells containing the therapeutic genes may die, or the genes may degenerate, and the diseases may reappear again as a result.

Legal issues:

  • Laws may be needed to prevent abuse / misuse of gene therapy.
  • Laws may be needed to ensure consent to gene therapy may be given only by a patient who is fully informed and capable of making decisions.

Social issues:

  • Cost of gene therapy is high, most people cannot afford treatment. Social inequality will become worse.

Economic issue:

  • Huge amount of money is spend on research.

Stem cell therapy

Main article: Stem cell therapy

Ethical issues:

  • Obtaining stem cells from early embryos results in the destruction of the embryos. It is controversial as it may be equivalent to killing human life, raising issues about whether or not embryos have human rights, and whether the sacrifice is justified.

Health issues:

  • Stem cells may divide uncontrollably and become cancerous in patients.
  • The cells grown from embryonic stem cells may cause an immune response and rejection in patients.

Legal issues:

  • Laws are needed to prevent usage in unauthorized procedures (e.g. human reproductive cloning).
  • Some people believe that embryos should not be created for the sole purpose of medical research.

Social issues:

  • Social inequality will worsen if only wealthy people can afford stem cell therapy.
  • Some people are concerned that the misuse of stem cells may lead to trading of embryos or the tissues or organs developed from stem cells.
