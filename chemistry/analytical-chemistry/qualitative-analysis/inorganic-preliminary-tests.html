Test for carbonate ion

Method 1:
Most carbonates (except and ) decompose upon heating, and give carbon dioxide gas as a product:

  • Heat the solid sample.
  • A colourless gas is given out, which turns limewater from colourless to milky.

Method 2:
Carbonates also reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to give carbon dioxide as a product:

  • Add into the acid.
  • A colourless gas is given out, which turns limewater from colourless to milky.
Warning

Beware that if the salt produced is insoluble, the test will not be feasible. As it will result in the formation of a barrier for the reactants that prevent further reaction.

Test for sulphur dioxide gas

Method 1:
Sulphur dioxide gas reacts with limewater to give insoluble calcium sulphite:

  • Pass the gas into limewater (until in excess).
  • A white precipitate is formed (and redissolves to form a colourless solution).

Method 2:
Sulphur dioxide gas is a reducing agent and reacts with an oxidizing agent (e.g. , , ) in acidic medium to give sulphate ions:

  • Put a filter paper soaked in acidified solution into the gas.
  • The paper turns from orange to green.

Test for sulphite ion

Method 1:
Sulphite ion is a reducing agent and reacts with an oxidizing agent (e.g. , , ) in acidic medium to give sulphate ions:

  • Add acidified solution into the solution sample.
  • The paper turns from orange to green.

Method 2:
Sulphite ion also reacts with acid (e.g. ) to give sulphur dioxide gas as the product:

  • Add into the solution sample.
  • A gas with choking smell of burning sulphur is formed, which turns acidified from orange to green.

Test for chlorine gas

Chlorine gas partially dissolves in water to give (acid) and (bleaching agent):

  • Put a moist blue litmus paper into the gas.
  • The paper turns from blue to red first, then to white.

Test for hypochlorite ion

Method 1:
Hypochlorite ion is a bleaching agent (due to its oxidizing power):

  • Add blue / red litmus solution into sample solution.
  • The solution turns from blue / red to colourless.
Tip

Although a piece of litmus paper would be bleached white, a solution would only turn colourless.

When iodine reacts with starch solution, a dark-blue complex is formed.

  • Put a filter paper soaked with starch solution.
  • The filter paper turns from white to dark-blue.

  • Add starch solution into solution sample.
  • The solution turns from brown to dark-blue / blue-black.
